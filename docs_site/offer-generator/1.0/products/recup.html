Recuperators

A recuperator recovers heat from a furnace’s hot waste flue gas and uses it to pre-heat the incoming combustion air. Pre-heated air means less fuel is burned, so the recuperator pays for itself in fuel savings. Unlike the catalogue products, a recuperator is sized from the heat duty — the system works out how big the tube bundle needs to be, then prices the steelwork.

What you enter

The heat duty.

  • The flue-gas flow and its inlet temperature.

  • The combustion-air flow, its inlet (ambient) temperature, and the target pre-heated temperature.

  • The heat-transfer coefficient (how readily heat passes through the tubes).

The tubes and steelwork.

  • Tube diameter, wall thickness, weight per metre and length per bank.

  • The gap between the hot and cold tube banks.

  • The material of the hot bank and the cold bank — stainless steel or mild steel (they can differ).

  • The side-hood weight, and an optional override for the air-inlet assembly rate.

  • An optional override to force a specific number of tubes.

Quantities and commercials. The order quantity, and the commercial figures: the cost-to-sale mark-up, packaging & forwarding, designing, negotiation, and a flat transport amount. Customer details, supervision charges and Terms & Conditions are entered for the offer.

What the offer contains

The sizing results — the heat to be recovered, the required heat-transfer surface area, the number of tubes and how they are arranged into rows and columns, the bank dimensions, and the weights of the tubes and the supporting steelwork.

A priced bill of materials — the hot-bank and cold-bank tubes, the side-hood fabrication, the combustion-air inlet assembly, the structural steel, the bending and welding labour, and the thermocouple — each priced and totalled into bought-out, ENCON and grand totals.

The offer document (Word + PDF), containing:

  • a Scope of Supply / Materials of Construction table describing the tubes (hot bank bent, cold bank straight) and the steel grades;

  • a Price Schedule — either a single all-inclusive line, or every item listed in full, as chosen;

  • a Designing Parameters table echoing the flue and air conditions, the surface area and the tube geometry;

  • optional 3-D images of the recuperator;

  • the standard Terms & Conditions.

How the price is worked out

  1. Heat balance. From the air flow and the temperature rise, work out how much heat must be recovered, and what temperature the flue gas drops to after giving up that heat.

  2. Surface area. Using the average temperature difference between the two streams and the heat-transfer coefficient, work out the heat-transfer surface area required.

  3. Tube count and layout. Convert the surface area into a number of tubes, then arrange them into a near-square grid of rows and columns, split evenly between the hot and cold banks.

  4. Dimensions and weights. From the grid, work out the bank length and width, and the weight of the tubes and of all the supporting steelwork.

  5. Price the materials. Price the tubes by weight at the stainless- or mild-steel rate, price the fabricated assemblies and structural steel, and add the bending, welding and drilling labour and the thermocouple — giving the grand total.

  6. Add the commercials. Apply the cost-to-sale mark-up, then add packaging & forwarding, designing and negotiation as percentages and the flat transport, rounding up to the nearest ₹1,000.

  7. Produce the documents — the price and all the sizing results go into the offer, which is saved as Word and PDF and filed.
